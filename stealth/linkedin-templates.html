Contrarian Take The Disconnected Organization
person Asaf Massuri (CEO / Strategic Voice)
AM

Asaf Massuri

Co-Founder & CEO | Rethinking Insider Risk

An employee downloads 50 files the week before resigning. Malicious or Monday? Your CISO can't tell. Neither can your DLP. The answer is in a conversation nobody's having. Here's what the insider risk industry got backwards: we built systems that make decisions in isolation. The DLP flags the download. The SOC analyst investigates alone. The CISO decides alone. Block or allow. Threat or false positive. Black or white. But insider risk is almost never black or white. That file download during notice period? The manager knows the employee is wrapping up a client handover. HR knows the departure is amicable and there's a 90-day garden leave clause. Legal knows the non-compete covers exactly this type of data. Each person holds a piece of the puzzle. None of them are in the room when the SOC analyst clicks "false positive" or "escalate." The failure isn't that we're watching employees. Every organization should understand what happens with its data. The failure is that the people with judgment, context, and authority are completely disconnected from the moment of decision. The CISO shouldn't decide alone. The DLP definitely shouldn't decide alone. And the SOC analyst staring at an alert at 3 AM shouldn't be making a call that requires knowing whether someone resigned, what project they're on, and whether their access still makes sense. The organizations getting insider risk right aren't the ones with the best monitoring. They're the ones where the right people are involved at the right time. That's a coordination problem, not a technology problem. What's the most underutilized connection in your insider risk workflow? #InsiderRisk #CISO #CyberSecurity #DataProtection

Hot Take The Signal Before the Signal
person Asaf Massuri (CEO / Strategic Voice)
AM

Asaf Massuri

Co-Founder & CEO | Rethinking Insider Risk

The data tells you someone is leaving before they tell their manager. Nobody is reading the signal. We talk about the "90 days before departure" as if departure is the starting gun. The employee resigns, HR flags it, security tightens access. That's the playbook most organizations run. But here's what actually happens in the data: weeks before anyone submits a resignation, behavioral patterns shift. The download rate doubles. Access to archived projects that haven't been touched in months suddenly spikes. Sharing patterns shift from team channels to personal email. Files get exported gradually, systematically, across days and weeks. The trajectory is visible. The signal is already there. The problem is that nobody is watching the trajectory. DLP sees individual events. An email with an attachment. A file downloaded from SharePoint. Each one reviewed in isolation. Each one "justified" on its own. But zoom out and the pattern is unmistakable: someone is collecting things. And by the time HR learns they're leaving, the collection is already complete. This is the gap nobody talks about: the behavioral signal arrives before the organizational signal. The data knows before the manager knows. But the industry is still waiting for HR to file a ticket before it starts paying attention. What if the question isn't "who has resigned" but "who is behaving like someone about to"? #CyberSecurity #InsiderThreat #CISO #SecurityLeadership

I've been having the same debate with CISOs for months, and I want to bring it here. When an employee does something risky with company data, you have a spectrum of responses: 1. Block it automatically. Fast. Decisive. But you'll block legitimate work too, and employees will find workarounds. 2. Alert the SOC and investigate. Thorough. But slow, expensive, and reactive. Most organizations have no playbook for this. Most security tools give you option 1 or option 2. Almost nobody is building for option 3. My take: the future of insider risk isn't about faster detection or harder blocking. It's about getting the right people involved at the right time, whether that's a manager having a conversation, HR reviewing a departure, or legal assessing exposure. But I know that's controversial because "orchestration doesn't scale." So I'll ask directly: if you could redesign how your organization handles insider risk from scratch, which of these three would you build around? Or is there a fourth option I'm missing? Framework Post The Three Questions Nobody's Asking
person Guy Hindi (CTO / Practitioner Voice)
GH

Guy Hindi

Co-Founder & CTO | Insider Risk, Security Operations

Your DLP flagged a file transfer. It says "sensitive data shared externally." But what does it actually know? Less than you think. Let's start with what DLP actually does when it fires an alert. It pattern-matches. Regex on credit card formats. Keywords like "confidential." File extensions that might contain source code. Sometimes it's right. Often it flags a test document with sample data, a public press release marked "internal," or a CSV of fake records from a dev environment. So even the WHAT, the one thing DLP is supposed to be good at, is unreliable. But let's say the content detection is perfect. Let's give it full credit for a moment. It correctly identifies a file with real customer records being shared externally. Alert fires. Now what? You still don't know WHO sent it. A ten-year veteran with a spotless record, or a contractor in their third week? You still don't know WHY. Legitimate client deliverable, or someone building a portfolio for their next job? You still don't know WHO received it. The client's project lead on the approved contacts list, or a personal Gmail? Until you know all three, you don't know if you have a problem or a Tuesday. Every insider risk decision comes down to WHO, WHY, and to WHOM. The industry has spent 20 years optimizing a content scanner that doesn't even do content well, while completely ignoring the three questions that actually determine risk. Which of these three is your biggest blind spot? #InsiderRisk #DLP #SecurityOperations #DataProtection

Execution Playbook

Posting Cadence (Ramp)

  • Weeks 1-2: 1 post per founder per week (2 total)
  • Weeks 3-6: 2 posts per founder per week (4 total)
  • Ongoing: 3-4 posts/week between both founders

Content Rotation (2-Week Cycle)

Week 1:

  • Asaf: Contrarian Take (Type 1)
  • Guy: DLP Series (Type 2) or Three Questions (Type 6)

Week 2:

  • Asaf: Hot Take or Question (Type 3/5)
  • Guy: Workflow Reality (Type 4)

Cross-Engagement Strategy

  • Asaf comments on Guy's posts (and vice versa)
  • Guy's practitioner posts validate Asaf's strategic claims
  • Asaf provides the "why it matters" frame for Guy's tactical observations
  • Creates a natural dialogue that feels authentic, not orchestrated

Engagement Rules

  • Respond to every comment within 4 hours
  • Never hint at a product. Redirect: "Just something I've been thinking about deeply."
  • Amplify commenters who share insider risk frustrations
  • Connect with every CISO/VP Security who engages

Visual Content Strategy

  • Stat cards: Bold number + short insight. Teal/dark palette. No branding.
  • Split-screen: "What DLP sees" vs "What it misses." Great for DLP Series.
  • Infographics: Simple math visuals (alerts x time = waste). For Workflow Reality.
  • Framework cards: Three Questions visual anchor. Reusable.
  • Timeline charts: Behavioral signal inflection before resignation. For Hot Take.
  • Stakeholder diagrams: Disconnected puzzle pieces. For Contrarian Take.
  • Video (future): 60-90s founder commentary on incidents. Authentic, not produced.

Success Metrics

  • Impressions/post: 2,000+ by week 4
  • Engagement rate: 3%+ (comments + shares)
  • Follower growth: 10-15% MoM per founder
  • Inbound DMs: Track CISOs reaching out
  • Ultimate signal: "What are you building?"

Do's and Don'ts

DO:

  • Use personal stories and industry observations
  • End with a question or invitation to engage
  • Use numbers from public sources only
  • Include a visual graphic with every post
  • Frame insider risk as gray, requiring judgment from multiple stakeholders

DON'T:

  • Mention AXIA, "our product," or "stay tuned"
  • Use em dashes (use periods, commas, colons)
  • Name specific competitors negatively
  • Use stock photography or generic "hacker" imagery
  • Give DLP undeserved credit for content detection
  • Frame insider risk as black/white (threat vs. not-threat)

V2 Core Themes

  • Insider risk is gray: Almost never black/white. Requires judgment from multiple stakeholders, not binary block/allow.
  • Behavioral signals are leading indicators: Data patterns predict intent before anyone files a resignation or tells a manager.
  • DLP is weaker than assumed: Don't concede content detection works. It doesn't. And even if it did, it misses WHO/WHY/WHOM.
  • Coordination > detection: The failure is organizational disconnection, not insufficient monitoring.
