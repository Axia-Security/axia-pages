Contrarian Take The Industry Is Broken
person Asaf Massuri (CEO / Strategic Voice)
AM

Asaf Massuri

Co-Founder & CEO | Rethinking Insider Risk

We've spent 20 years building insider risk programs that treat employees like threats. And we're surprised they don't work. Think about what the industry has built: systems that monitor every keystroke, flag every file transfer, block every USB port. We created surveillance infrastructure and called it "data protection." The result? 70-90% false positive rates. Security teams drowning in alerts they can't investigate. Employees who feel watched but never heard. And the insiders who actually intend harm? They learned to work around the rules years ago. Here's what nobody in the boardroom wants to admit: the problem isn't detection. We can detect everything. The problem is that detection without judgment is just noise. And noise at scale is paralysis. The next generation of insider risk won't be defined by who can monitor the most. It will be defined by who can understand the most. That's a fundamentally different challenge. What's the biggest gap you see in how organizations handle insider risk today? #InsiderRisk #CISO #CyberSecurity #DataProtection

Hot Take Incident Commentary
person Asaf Massuri (CEO / Strategic Voice)
AM

Asaf Massuri

Co-Founder & CEO | Rethinking Insider Risk

90% of insider data theft happens in the 90 days before an employee leaves. Your DLP doesn't know someone is leaving. HR does. I've been watching this cycle for 15 years. Incident happens. Vendor publishes a blog post about how their tool would have caught it. CISO buys another license. Nothing fundamentally changes. Here's what we keep getting wrong: we analyze these incidents as technology failures. "The DLP should have caught it." "The access controls were too permissive." But the real failure is almost never technical. It's organizational. The employee who exfiltrates data before leaving doesn't do it because the firewall had a gap. They do it because nobody discussed what was appropriate during their notice period. Nobody checked whether their access still matched their changing role. Nobody engaged the right people at the right time. We keep building better locks. The problem is that we're not having better conversations. What would change if we treated insider risk as a people problem first and a technology problem second? #CyberSecurity #InsiderThreat #CISO #SecurityLeadership

Monday morning. 1,247 DLP alerts from the weekend. One real threat. Your team has 8 hours. Go. Let me walk you through what that looks like in practice. Alert fires at 2:47 PM. Analyst opens the ticket. Checks the user's role. Checks the file classification. Checks the recipient. Pulls up the user's history. Reads the email for context. That's over 20 hours of daily investigation time consumed by noise. In a team of five analysts, that's the equivalent of 2.5 full-time employees doing nothing but confirming that legitimate business activities are, in fact, legitimate. And here's the part that keeps me up at night: while those analysts are drowning in false positives, the genuinely risky activity sits in the queue. Waiting. Because the system doesn't know the difference between a sales rep doing their job and a departing engineer collecting IP. It treats them exactly the same way. We don't have a detection problem. We have a prioritization problem. And the industry's answer is to hire more analysts to look at more noise. If you run a SOC or security team, how do you handle alert fatigue today? Genuinely curious. #SecOps #DLP #AlertFatigue #CyberSecurity

Engagement Spark Discussion
person Asaf Massuri (alternating)
AM

Asaf Massuri

Co-Founder & CEO | Rethinking Insider Risk

I've been having the same debate with CISOs for months, and I want to bring it here. When an employee does something risky with company data, you have a spectrum of responses: 1. Block it automatically. Fast. Decisive. But you'll block legitimate work too, and employees will find workarounds. 2. Alert the SOC and investigate. Thorough. But slow, expensive, and reactive. The data may already be gone. 3. Engage the right people. Loop in the manager, HR, or legal depending on the situation. But who decides whom to involve? Based on what? Most organizations have no playbook for this. Most security tools give you option 1 or option 2. Almost nobody is building for option 3. My take: the future of insider risk isn't about faster detection or harder blocking. It's about getting the right people involved at the right time, whether that's a manager having a conversation, HR reviewing a departure, or legal assessing exposure. But I know that's controversial because "orchestration doesn't scale." So I'll ask directly: if you could redesign how your organization handles insider risk from scratch, which of these three would you build around? Or is there a fourth option I'm missing? Drop your take below. I'll respond to every comment. #InsiderRisk #CISO #SecurityStrategy #CyberSecurity

Framework Post The Three Questions Nobody's Asking
person Guy Hindi (CTO / Practitioner Voice)
GH

Guy Hindi

Co-Founder & CTO | Insider Risk, Security Operations

Your DLP can tell you exactly what was in that file. Credit card numbers. Social Security numbers. Source code. But it can't answer three questions that matter more. Who sent it? Why did they send it? Who received it? Until you know all three, you don't know if you have a problem or a Tuesday. Think about it. A file containing customer records gets shared externally. Your DLP fires an alert for "sensitive data shared outside the organization." That's the WHAT. Now consider the three questions: WHO sent it? A contractor who's been with the company for six months. WHY did they send it? They're delivering a client report that requires sample data. WHO received it? The client's project lead, on the approved contacts list. Same alert. Zero risk. But without those three answers, your analyst just burned 30 minutes to reach a conclusion the system should have surfaced in seconds. Now change one variable. The sender submitted their resignation yesterday. Same file. Same recipient. Completely different risk profile. The WHAT didn't change. The WHO did. Every insider risk decision comes down to these three questions. The industry has spent 20 years optimizing for WHAT. Almost nobody is asking WHO and WHY. Which of these three does your current setup handle best? Which one is your blind spot? #InsiderRisk #DLP #SecurityOperations #DataProtection

Execution Playbook

Posting Cadence (Ramp)

  • Weeks 1-2: 1 post per founder per week (2 total)
  • Weeks 3-6: 2 posts per founder per week (4 total)
  • Ongoing: 3-4 posts/week between both founders

Content Rotation (2-Week Cycle)

Week 1:

  • Asaf: Contrarian Take (Type 1)
  • Guy: DLP Series (Type 2) or Three Questions (Type 6)

Week 2:

  • Asaf: Hot Take or Question (Type 3/5)
  • Guy: Workflow Reality (Type 4)

Cross-Engagement Strategy

  • Asaf comments on Guy's posts (and vice versa)
  • Guy's practitioner posts validate Asaf's strategic claims
  • Asaf provides the "why it matters" frame for Guy's tactical observations
  • Creates a natural dialogue that feels authentic, not orchestrated

Engagement Rules

  • Respond to every comment within 4 hours
  • Never hint at a product. Redirect: "Just something I've been thinking about deeply."
  • Amplify commenters who share insider risk frustrations
  • Connect with every CISO/VP Security who engages

Visual Content Strategy

  • Stat cards: Bold number + short insight. Teal/dark palette. No branding.
  • Split-screen: "What DLP sees" vs "What it misses." Great for DLP Series.
  • Infographics: Simple math visuals (alerts x time = waste). For Workflow Reality.
  • Framework cards: Three Questions visual anchor. Reusable.
  • Video (future): 60-90s founder commentary on incidents. Authentic, not produced.

Success Metrics

  • Impressions/post: 2,000+ by week 4
  • Engagement rate: 3%+ (comments + shares)
  • Follower growth: 10-15% MoM per founder
  • Inbound DMs: Track CISOs reaching out
  • Ultimate signal: "What are you building?"

Do's and Don'ts

DO:

  • Use personal stories and industry observations
  • End with a question or invitation to engage
  • Use numbers from public sources only
  • Include a visual graphic with every post

DON'T:

  • Mention AXIA, "our product," or "stay tuned"
  • Use em dashes (use periods, commas, colons)
  • Name specific competitors negatively
  • Use stock photography or generic "hacker" imagery

Data Hook Bank (Stealth-Safe Stats)

  • "1,247 alerts. Monday morning. One real threat."
  • "90% of data theft happens in the 90 days before departure."
  • "72% of orgs run 2+ DLP solutions. None talk to each other."
  • "85% of insider incidents involve legitimate access."
  • "86 days average to contain an insider incident."
  • "$16.2M average cost of an insider-caused incident."
  • "70-90% false positive rates in DLP deployments."
