The Challenge

Every document could be a risk.
Most aren't.

AXIA needs to scan massive volumes of corporate communications, but only a fraction contain sensitive data. Sending everything through an LLM would be prohibitively expensive. Skipping the LLM would miss critical context.

⚡

Speed

Thousands of documents per minute. Classification must be near-instant.

💰

Cost

LLM calls are expensive. You can't run GPT-4 on every Slack message.

🎯

Accuracy

Missing a sensitive document is a compliance failure. False positives erode trust.

The solution: a two-stage architecture that's fast by default and deep when it matters.

04 / 12
The Ensemble

Three Models.
One Vote.

Three architecturally different classifiers run in parallel. Their predictions are combined through confidence-weighted soft voting — not simple majority.

SetFit
sentence-transformer
Few-shot fine-tuned. Lightweight, fast inference. Good at learning from small labeled datasets.
ModernBERT
transformer encoder
Modern BERT variant. Strong contextual understanding with long-sequence support. The backbone.
Embeddings + XGBoost/NN
traditional ML
Embedding representations fed to gradient boosting or neural net. Interpretable. Different failure modes.

Why three? Each model architecture has different blind spots. A transformer might miss tabular PII that XGBoost catches from structural features. SetFit may catch patterns BERT needs more data to learn. The ensemble's disagreement is itself a signal.

📑 Tabular Output (CSV)

Structured records: trade data, client lists, account information, payment details. Headers, CUSIP IDs, dollar amounts.

📝 Textual Output (Markdown)

Unstructured content: emails, memos, reports, compliance documents. Natural language with embedded sensitive data.

Key design choice: Three different LLMs generate the synthetic data (Gemini, Claude, GPT). This avoids stylistic monoculture — if one model has a "tell," the others won't share it.

10 / 12
What's Working

Solid Foundations.
Smart Tradeoffs.

📈

Ensemble Diversity

Three architecturally different classifiers mean different failure modes. Where one is blind, another sees.

🧬

Real + Synthetic Mix

Enron and Slack provide authentic ground truth. Synthetic fills the financial domain gap that can't be sourced publicly.

🤖

Multi-Model Generation

Gemini + Claude + GPT for synthetic data. No single model's stylistic fingerprint dominates the training set.

💸

Cost-Efficient Inference

The LLM only fires on flagged documents. Fast classifiers handle the bulk cheaply. This is the right design for scale.

✅

Validation Loop

Optional LLM validator catches low-quality synthetic data before it pollutes the training set.

🏢

Domain Fit

Topic categories are well-chosen for financial IRM: payroll, wire transfers, PII documents, compliance.

11 / 12
Discussion Points

Questions
for Orr

Areas to explore in our next conversation.

Q1
Ensemble Calibration
How are soft voting weights determined? Static or learned from a validation set? How often recalibrated?
Q2
Chunk Boundaries
What happens when context spans chunks? A name in one chunk, SSN in the next. Is there overlap or cross-chunk attention?
Q3
Base Rate Mismatch
Synthetic data is 88% sensitive. Production will be mostly clean. Is the model calibrated for real-world base rates?
Q4
PII Label Gap
63% of records have no PII labels. Is PII detection a separate model or derived from sensitivity classification?
Q5
Slack Volume
Only 3K Slack records. With Slack/Teams connectors in the JIRA backlog, this source needs significant expansion.
Q6
Data Freshness
Enron is from 2000-2001. Modern comms (emojis, links, threads) may not be represented. Plan for modern email corpora?
Q7
Topic Coverage
63% of records are topic-unlabeled. Does this hurt topic-aware classifiers or is topic only used in the LLM analysis stage?
Q8
Connector-Specific Tuning
Will ensemble weights vary by connector type? Email vs. Slack vs. file share have very different data shapes.
