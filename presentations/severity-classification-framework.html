AXIA Severity Classification Framework

Version: 1.0 Date: 2026-02-26 Purpose: Codified classification logic for insider risk severity, derived from analysis of 150 ground-truth examples. Used as both an internal reference and the LLM benchmark prompt guide.

How Severity Is Determined

AXIA's severity classification is driven by three interacting dimensions (the WHO/WHAT/WHO framework), not any single factor in isolation:

  1. WHO sent it — Role, access level, behavioral signals (departure, grievance, financial stress), intent indicators
  2. WHAT it contains — Data type, regulatory classification, competitive sensitivity, volume
  3. WHO received it — Relationship to sender, authorization level, organizational controls in place
Severity emerges from the combination of these dimensions. The same data sent to different recipients can be different severities. The same recipient receiving different data types can be different severities. Behavioral context (departure, grievance, financial pressure) acts as an amplifier that can push a case up one level.

Severity Levels

CRITICAL (Score 90-100)

Response: Preserve evidence, notify Legal, escalate to CISO. Immediate human confirmation required.

Core pattern: Active or imminent harm to the organization, its customers, or the financial system, involving either (a) deliberate malicious action, (b) regulatory violations carrying criminal or systemic consequences, or (c) exposure of data whose compromise could cause irreversible damage.

Key CRITICAL signals:

  • Recipient is personal email, competitor, unauthorized external party, dark web, or anonymous/encrypted channel
  • Data is regulated (PCI DSS, GLBA, BSA/AML, SEC, biometric privacy laws)
  • Sender shows departure + competitor destination, or financial distress + privileged access
  • Deliberate concealment methods (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing)
  • Criminal liability potential (securities fraud, wire fraud, sanctions violations, data breach reporting obligations)

HIGH (Score 70-89)

Response: Notify manager, preserve evidence. Manager-level review and action required.

Core pattern: Significant data exposure or policy violation that creates material business risk, but with one or more mitigating factors that reduce immediacy compared to CRITICAL: the recipient has some legitimate relationship, the data lacks the most sensitive regulatory classifications, the sender shows no malicious intent (though may show poor judgment), or the exposure has not yet left organizational control.

What distinguishes HIGH from CRITICAL:

  • Data is commercially sensitive but not regulated PII, not MNPI, not criminal
  • Recipient has some existing relationship (vendor, partner, board member) even if authorization is missing
  • Sender intent is not malicious (though may be self-serving, negligent, or pressure-driven)
  • Exposure is contained or recoverable (still within company systems, or sent to a single known party)
  • No criminal liability, but material business harm possible
What distinguishes HIGH from MEDIUM:
  • Data volume or sensitivity is materially higher
  • Recipient is external (not internal misdirection)
  • Behavioral risk signals are present (departure, grievance, financial pressure, undisclosed conflicts)
  • Governance bypass is deliberate rather than accidental
  • Business impact potential is significant (competitive intelligence, client poaching, regulatory risk)

MEDIUM (Score 50-69)

Response: Coach employee. Educational engagement, process correction.

Core pattern: Policy violations driven by carelessness, ignorance, poor process, or misguided helpfulness rather than malicious intent. The data has some sensitivity but the exposure is limited, internal, or to parties with partial authorization. Correction through coaching is appropriate because the root cause is behavioral/procedural, not adversarial.

Key MEDIUM signals:

  • Sender has no malicious intent (careless, helpful, ignorant, pressured, or building personal brand)
  • Data is sensitive but not the most regulated categories
  • Recipient often has partial legitimacy (vendor with relationship but missing DPA, internal but wrong department, public but limited audience)
  • Behavioral risk signals are absent or mild
  • Problem is fixable through coaching and process improvement
  • No deliberate concealment or DLP evasion

LOW (Score 0-49)

Response: Auto-resolve if matches known-good pattern. Verify and close.

Core pattern: Legitimate business activity that was correctly flagged by a pattern-matching DLP system but does not represent actual risk upon contextual analysis. The data is authorized for sharing, the recipient is appropriate, and the activity aligns with the sender's role and organizational policies.

Key LOW signals:

  • Content is already public, approved for distribution, or designed for sharing
  • Recipient is authorized (engaged counsel, contracted vendor, approved partner, internal team)
  • Activity aligns with sender's role responsibilities
  • No risk factors in sender profile
  • DLP flagged on keyword patterns, but contextual analysis shows no actual risk

Boundary Decisions: What Makes the Difference

CRITICAL vs. HIGH boundary

The dividing line is primarily regulatory/criminal exposure and recipient authorization:

FactorTips toward CRITICALTips toward HIGH
Data typeRegulated (PCI, GLBA, BSA/AML, SEC, biometric)Commercial/competitive (client lists, pricing, roadmap)
Regulatory consequenceCriminal liability, reportable breach, SEC violationPolicy violation, contractual breach, NDA violation
RecipientNo relationship, competitor, anonymous, dark webExisting relationship but missing authorization
IntentDeliberate exfiltration, concealment, fraudNegligent, pressure-driven, self-serving
Sender signalsDeparture + competitor, financial distress, prior violationsDeparture alone, work pressure, career ambition
ReversibilityIrreversible (data in the wild, crypto keys compromised)Potentially recoverable (known recipient, NDA possible)

HIGH vs. MEDIUM boundary

The dividing line is external vs. internal, behavioral signals, and business impact scale:

FactorTips toward HIGHTips toward MEDIUM
RecipientExternal (competitor, vendor, public)Internal (wrong department, over-broad access)
Sender signalsDeparture, grievance, financial conflict, undisclosed interestsNone, or mild (eagerness, inexperience, pressure)
Governance bypassDeliberate, aware of the requirementAccidental or ignorance-based
Data volume/impactBulk export, complete portfolios, production credentialsLimited scope, single documents, operational data
ConcealmentSome evidence (off-hours, encrypted email, mislabeling)None (sender often openly discusses the sharing)

MEDIUM vs. LOW boundary

The dividing line is whether any actual policy violation occurred:

FactorTips toward MEDIUMTips toward LOW
AuthorizationMissing or incomplete (no DPA, no pre-review, wrong channel)Fully authorized (approved, privileged, contractual)
Content sensitivityContains some sensitive data that shouldn't be shared this wayAlready public, personal, or designed for distribution
Process complianceProcess was violated (wrong recipient, wrong channel, missing anonymization)Process was followed (approved, reviewed, proper channel)
Risk factorsMild but present (new employee, pressure, personal brand ambitions)None

Amplifiers and Mitigators

These contextual factors shift severity up or down from where the data type alone would suggest:

Amplifiers (push severity UP):

  • Confirmed departure to competitor
  • Financial distress or personal grievance
  • Deliberate concealment (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing)
  • Pattern of escalation (multiple incidents, increasing volume)
  • Undisclosed conflicts of interest
  • Prior disciplinary history for similar behavior
Mitigators (push severity DOWN):
  • Recipient has existing authorized relationship (vendor with NDA, engaged law firm)
  • Data stayed within organizational systems (self-email, internal misdirection)
  • Clear accidental nature (autocomplete error, wrong channel)
  • Sender proactively disclosed or sought approval
  • Data volume is limited (single document vs. bulk export)
  • Content was already public or approaching public release
