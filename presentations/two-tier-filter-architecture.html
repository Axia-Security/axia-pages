The Problem

The Insight

Conservative error = perfect pre-filter

A classifier that over-flags is annoying. But a classifier that never misses real risk while correctly identifying all the noise? That's a production-grade filter.

It doesn't need to understand context. It doesn't need to assess intent. It just needs to answer one question:

"Is there anything here worth thinking about?"

Enhancement 1 — Simple Version

Fix the wrong grades

The Analogy

Imagine a teacher grading homework. She marks 38 answers "wrong" when they're actually right. Not because the students made mistakes, but because the teacher's answer key is outdated.

We found 38 of those. 22 emails labeled "Low risk" that are actually just people chatting about their day. 16 labeled "Medium" that are developers talking about code.

The fix: correct the answer key. No new homework needed. Just stop penalizing the right answers.

This is the quickest win. Zero new data, just corrected labels on samples we already have.

Enhancement 2 — Simple Version

Stop the three silly mistakes

The Analogy

The system makes three types of silly mistakes, like a paranoid security guard:

Mistake A: Anyone who says the word "money" gets stopped. "Hey, did you see that movie about a bank robbery?" — DETAINED. The guard thinks talking about money = stealing money.

Mistake B: Anything that sounds complicated must be secret. A recipe in French? Must be a spy code. A developer discussing Kubernetes? Must be hacking.

Mistake C: Anything from work must be important. Every email from the office is treated like a classified document — even the lunch menu.

The fix: show the guard thousands of harmless examples of each. "See? People talk about money, use technical words, and send work emails all day. That's normal."

Enhancement 2 — The Evidence

The three failure patterns in detail

Pattern A: Topic Confusion
"Meeting to discuss Q3 energy trading strategy and regulatory compliance"
Training: HIGH (trading + regulatory)
AXIA: NON-SENSITIVE (meeting, no data)

Fix: Add policy docs, compliance discussions, financial planning emails labeled non-sensitive.

Pattern B: Complexity Bias
"Deployed Flyte workflow for k8s pod scaling. Updated IAM roles for staging."
Training: MEDIUM (technical)
AXIA: NON-SENSITIVE (routine DevOps)

Fix: Add code reviews, architecture docs, technical RFCs.

Pattern C: Business Assumption
"Updated project timeline for the client deliverable. Review before Friday standup."
Training: LOW (business content)
AXIA: NON-SENSITIVE (project email)

Fix: Add meeting agendas, status updates, project plans.

Enhancement 3 — Simple Version

Teach the difference between talking and doing

The Analogy

A museum security guard stops everyone who says the word "painting." The art teacher explaining Monet to students? Stopped. The tour guide describing a Picasso? Stopped. Meanwhile, a thief silently walks out with a canvas under their coat — no alarm, because they never said the word.

Right now, our classifier is that guard. It flags every email that mentions payroll, credentials, or trading. But it ignores a coded message like "the garden is blooming, time to harvest" because it doesn't contain any flagged words — even though it's insider trading in disguise.

The fix: teach the guard that hearing "painting" is different from someone carrying one out the door. Same word, completely different situation.

This is the single highest-impact enhancement. It addresses the fundamental gap between content classification and behavioral risk.

Enhancement 3 — The Evidence

Build the "about vs. contains" dataset

Same Document, Two Realities

A payroll file is Critical in training data (has SSNs). Under AXIA: Low when HR accesses internally, Critical when a departing employee exports 1,247 records to Gmail at 2 AM.

The Inverse Problem

"The garden is blooming, time to harvest." Zero PII. Training says Low. AXIA recognizes Critical: coded insider trading about executing on material non-public information.

What to Build

Contrastive pairs: same topic, different content. Salary policy discussion (safe) vs. salary spreadsheet (sensitive). Security training doc (safe) vs. credential dump (critical).

Missing Behavioral Signals
