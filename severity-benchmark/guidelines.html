AXIA Detailed Severity Guidelines

AXIA's comprehensive severity classification framework for insider risk incidents. Uses the WHO/WHAT/WHO methodology: who sent it, what it contains, who received it. Includes violation category tables for each tier, boundary decision matrices, and amplifier/mitigator lists.

CRITICAL (Score 90-100)

Response: Requires immediate alerting/visibility of executive compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). Immediate human confirmation required.

Core pattern: Active or imminent harm involving either (a) deliberate malicious action, (b) regulatory violations carrying criminal or systemic consequences, or (c) exposure of data whose compromise could cause irreversible damage.

Violation Categories
CategoryWhat It Looks Like
IP exfiltration to competitorDeparting employee sends proprietary code, algorithms, or trade secrets to personal email or directly to competitor, especially with departure + competitor destination confirmed
Regulatory crime / securities violationPre-earnings data to investors, insider trading facilitation, sanctions evasion, SAR disclosure, wire fraud
Customer PII/financial data theftBulk export of customer records (PII, payment data, biometrics) to personal or external destinations, especially with concealment or disguise
Infrastructure/credential compromiseProduction API keys, SWIFT credentials, cold wallet keys, core banking credentials sent externally or posted to dark web
DLP evasion / deliberate concealmentEncryption specifically to bypass scanning, disguised filenames, audit trail manipulation
MNPI disclosureMaterial non-public information shared before authorized release to unauthorized parties
Fraud / financial crime by insiderWire redirection, forged documents, bribery, collusion with external bad actors
Key Signals
  • Recipient is personal email, competitor, unauthorized external party, dark web, or anonymous/encrypted channel
  • Data is regulated (PCI DSS, GLBA, BSA/AML, SEC, biometric privacy laws)
  • Sender shows departure + competitor destination, or financial distress + privileged access
  • Deliberate concealment methods (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing)
  • Criminal liability potential (securities fraud, wire fraud, sanctions violations)

HIGH (Score 70-89)

Response: Requires manager and/or alerting/visibility of operational compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). Manager-level review and action required.

Core pattern: Significant data exposure or policy violation that creates material business risk, but with one or more mitigating factors: the recipient has some legitimate relationship, the data lacks the most sensitive regulatory classifications, the sender shows no malicious intent, or the exposure has not yet left organizational control.

Violation Categories
CategoryWhat It Looks Like
Commercial intelligence to competitorClient lists, loan portfolios, pricing, CRM data sent to competitors or personal storage by departing employees - data is commercial rather than regulated PII
Sensitive data to legitimate-but-unauthorized recipientsData shared with vendors, partners, or investors who have a business relationship but lack proper authorization (no NDA, no DPA, outside contract scope)
Internal data at massive scale / misconfigurationLarge-volume customer data exposed through misconfiguration or automation, remaining within organizational systems but far exceeding appropriate access
Governance bypass with sensitive dataWell-intentioned sharing that bypasses required approval processes when the data is materially sensitive
Compliance/regulatory data shared with inappropriate scopeAML thresholds, transaction monitoring rules, examination findings shared with parties who should not have them
Personal use of institutional resources for financial gainUsing corporate terminals, data, or access for personal trading, investment research, or side businesses
Sensitive data to media / public platform with significant impactFeature flags revealing roadmap to journalist, operational data to PR agency during crisis, calendar/travel data to recruiter
What Distinguishes HIGH from CRITICAL
  • Data is commercially sensitive but not regulated PII, not MNPI, not criminal
  • Recipient has some existing relationship (vendor, partner, board member) even if authorization is missing
  • Sender intent is not malicious (though may be self-serving, negligent, or pressure-driven)
  • Exposure is contained or recoverable (still within company systems, or sent to a single known party)
  • No criminal liability, but material business harm possible

MEDIUM (Score 50-69)

Response: Requires employee engagement and/or coaching for resolution. Educational engagement, process correction.

Core pattern: Policy violations driven by carelessness, ignorance, poor process, or misguided helpfulness rather than malicious intent. The data has some sensitivity but the exposure is limited, internal, or to parties with partial authorization.

Violation Categories
CategoryWhat It Looks Like
Accidental misdirection / wrong recipientEmail to wrong client, reply-all with sensitive data, posting to wrong channel - clear human error with no malicious intent
Over-sharing with legitimate intentEmployee shares more data than necessary for a legitimate purpose, or shares via inappropriate channel
Process/policy violation without maliceBypassing proper channels, using wrong tools, failing to follow data handling procedures - coaching will fix it
Data in unsanctioned personal toolsEmployees copying work data to personal Notion, blogs, GitHub, social media - not for theft but for productivity or personal brand building
Legitimate relationship, premature or excessive sharingSharing with auditors, regulators, board candidates, or partners before proper governance review
Internal boundary violationsAccessing data outside assigned portfolio, sharing across internal information barriers, credential sharing between colleagues
Public or semi-public disclosure of operational detailsSharing operational metrics, internal processes, or competitive details in podcasts, blogs, conferences, or developer communities
Key MEDIUM Signals
  • Sender has no malicious intent (careless, helpful, ignorant, pressured, or building personal brand)
  • Data is sensitive but not the most regulated categories
  • Recipient often has partial legitimacy (vendor with relationship but missing DPA, internal but wrong department, public but limited audience)
  • Behavioral risk signals are absent or mild
  • Problem is fixable through coaching and process improvement
  • No deliberate concealment or DLP evasion

LOW (Score 0-49)

Response: Auto-resolve if matches known-good pattern. Verify and close.

Core pattern: Legitimate business activity that was correctly flagged by a pattern-matching DLP system but does not represent actual risk. The data is authorized for sharing, the recipient is appropriate, and the activity aligns with the sender's role.

Categories (False Positives)
CategoryWhat It Looks Like
Pre-approved content sharingMarketing collateral, published reports, approved case studies, public filings sent to appropriate recipients
Public/regulatory documentsPublicly filed documents, open-source content, government publications, industry standards
Authorized external collaborationSharing with engaged law firms (privilege), contracted vendors with DPA, approved academic partnerships
Routine internal operationsStandard management reporting, internal escalations, training materials using public references
Personal content triggering keyword matchesPersonal financial planning, tax documents, personal LinkedIn articles - mentions employer but contains no company data
Departing employee - personal files onlyDLP flags departure + data transfer, but content is personal (resume, photos, reviews)

Amplifiers & Mitigators

Amplifiers (push severity UP)
  • Confirmed departure to competitor
  • Financial distress or personal grievance
  • Deliberate concealment (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing)
  • Pattern of escalation (multiple incidents, increasing volume)
  • Undisclosed conflicts of interest
  • Prior disciplinary history for similar behavior
Mitigators (push severity DOWN)
  • Recipient has existing authorized relationship (vendor with NDA, engaged law firm)
  • Data stayed within organizational systems (self-email, internal misdirection)
  • Clear accidental nature (autocomplete error, wrong channel)
  • Sender proactively disclosed or sought approval
  • Data volume is limited (single document vs. bulk export)
  • Content was already public or approaching public release
