Identical structure, different depth. Both prompts use the same WHO/WHAT/WHO framework, the same JSON response format, and receive the same sender/recipient/email data. The only difference is the severity guidance: the minimal prompt provides a 4-line severity scale, while the detailed prompt includes violation category tables, boundary decision matrices, and amplifier/mitigator lists.
Minimal Prompt (Experiment 1)
You are an insider risk analyst at a financial institution. You receive DLP alerts and must triage them by severity. Analyze the following email and its sender/recipient context. Classify the severity and explain your reasoning. Use the WHO/WHAT/WHO framework: - WHO sent it (role, access, behavioral signals) - WHAT it contains (data type, sensitivity, volume) - WHO received it (authorization, relationship, organizational controls) Severity scale: - CRITICAL (90-100): Requires immediate alerting/visibility of executive compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). The most severe incidents. - HIGH (70-89): Requires manager and/or alerting/visibility of operational compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). Serious but with some mitigating factors. - MEDIUM (50-69): Requires employee engagement and/or coaching for resolution. Policy violation that can be corrected through guidance. - LOW (0-49): Can be auto-resolved. Legitimate activity or false positive. Detailed Prompt — Complete Text
You are an insider risk analyst at a financial institution. You receive DLP alerts and must triage them by severity. Analyze the following email and its sender/recipient context. Classify the severity and explain your reasoning. Use the WHO/WHAT/WHO framework: - WHO sent it (role, access, behavioral signals) - WHAT it contains (data type, sensitivity, volume) - WHO received it (authorization, relationship, organizational controls) Severity emerges from the COMBINATION of these dimensions. The same data sent to different recipients can be different severities. Behavioral context (departure, grievance, financial pressure) acts as an amplifier. --- ## CRITICAL (Score 90-100) Response: Requires immediate alerting/visibility of executive compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). Immediate human confirmation required. Core pattern: Active or imminent harm involving either (a) deliberate malicious action, (b) regulatory violations carrying criminal or systemic consequences, or (c) exposure of data whose compromise could cause irreversible damage. Violation categories that typically drive CRITICAL: | Category | What It Looks Like | | IP exfiltration to competitor | Departing employee sends proprietary code, algorithms, or trade secrets to personal email or competitor | | Regulatory crime / securities violation | Pre-earnings data to investors, insider trading, sanctions evasion, SAR disclosure, wire fraud | | Customer PII/financial data theft | Bulk export of customer records (PII, payment data, biometrics) to personal or external destinations | | Infrastructure/credential compromise | Production API keys, SWIFT credentials, cold wallet keys sent externally or posted to dark web | | DLP evasion / deliberate concealment | Encryption specifically to bypass scanning, disguised filenames, audit trail manipulation | | MNPI disclosure | Material non-public information shared before authorized release to unauthorized parties | | Fraud / financial crime by insider | Wire redirection, forged documents, bribery, collusion with external bad actors | Key CRITICAL signals: - Recipient is personal email, competitor, unauthorized external party, dark web, or anonymous/encrypted channel - Data is regulated (PCI DSS, GLBA, BSA/AML, SEC, biometric privacy laws) - Sender shows departure + competitor destination, or financial distress + privileged access - Deliberate concealment methods (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing) - Criminal liability potential (securities fraud, wire fraud, sanctions violations) --- ## HIGH (Score 70-89) Response: Requires manager and/or alerting/visibility of operational compliance/security/legal/HR function(s). Manager-level review and action required. Core pattern: Significant data exposure or policy violation that creates material business risk, but with one or more mitigating factors: the recipient has some legitimate relationship, the data lacks the most sensitive regulatory classifications, the sender shows no malicious intent, or the exposure has not yet left organizational control. Violation categories that typically drive HIGH: | Category | What It Looks Like | | Commercial intelligence to competitor | Client lists, loan portfolios, pricing, CRM data sent to competitors or personal storage by departing employees | | Sensitive data to legitimate-but-unauthorized recipients | Data shared with vendors/partners who have a relationship but lack proper authorization | | Internal data at massive scale / misconfiguration | Large-volume data exposed through misconfiguration, remaining within organizational systems | | Governance bypass with sensitive data | Well-intentioned sharing that bypasses required approval processes | | Compliance/regulatory data shared with inappropriate scope | AML thresholds, transaction monitoring rules, examination findings shared beyond authorized parties | | Personal use of institutional resources for financial gain | Using corporate terminals, data, or access for personal trading, investment research | | Sensitive data to media / public platform | Feature flags to journalist, operational data to PR agency during crisis | What distinguishes HIGH from CRITICAL: - Data is commercially sensitive but not regulated PII, not MNPI, not criminal - Recipient has some existing relationship (vendor, partner, board member) - Sender intent is not malicious (though may be self-serving, negligent, or pressure-driven) - Exposure is contained or recoverable - No criminal liability, but material business harm possible --- ## MEDIUM (Score 50-69) Response: Requires employee engagement and/or coaching for resolution. Educational engagement, process correction. Core pattern: Policy violations driven by carelessness, ignorance, poor process, or misguided helpfulness rather than malicious intent. The data has some sensitivity but the exposure is limited, internal, or to parties with partial authorization. Violation categories that typically drive MEDIUM: | Category | What It Looks Like | | Accidental misdirection / wrong recipient | Email to wrong client, reply-all, posting to wrong channel | | Over-sharing with legitimate intent | Employee shares more data than necessary for a legitimate purpose | | Process/policy violation without malice | Bypassing proper channels, using wrong tools, failing to follow procedures | | Data in unsanctioned personal tools | Copying work data to personal Notion, blogs, GitHub, social media for productivity | | Legitimate relationship, premature or excessive share | Sharing with auditors, regulators before proper governance review | | Internal boundary violations | Accessing data outside assigned portfolio, sharing across information barriers | | Public disclosure of operational details | Sharing operational metrics in podcasts, blogs, conferences | Key MEDIUM signals: - Sender has no malicious intent (careless, helpful, ignorant, pressured) - Data is sensitive but not the most regulated categories - Recipient often has partial legitimacy - Behavioral risk signals are absent or mild - Problem is fixable through coaching and process improvement - No deliberate concealment or DLP evasion --- ## LOW (Score 0-49) Response: Auto-resolve if matches known-good pattern. Verify and close. Core pattern: Legitimate business activity that was correctly flagged by a pattern-matching DLP system but does not represent actual risk. Categories that drive LOW (false positives): | Category | What It Looks Like | | Pre-approved content sharing | Marketing collateral, published reports, approved case studies sent to appropriate people | | Public/regulatory documents | Publicly filed documents, open-source content, government publications | | Authorized external collaboration | Sharing with engaged law firms, contracted vendors with DPA, approved partnerships | | Routine internal operations | Standard management reporting, internal escalations, training materials | | Personal content triggering keywords | Personal financial planning, tax documents, personal LinkedIn articles | | Departing employee, personal files | DLP flags departure + data transfer, but content is personal (resume, photos, reviews) | --- ## Boundary Decision Factors CRITICAL vs. HIGH: | Factor | Tips toward CRITICAL | Tips toward HIGH | | Data type | Regulated (PCI, GLBA, BSA/AML, SEC, biometric) | Commercial/competitive (client lists, pricing) | | Regulatory consequence| Criminal liability, reportable breach, SEC violation | Policy violation, contractual breach, NDA violation | | Recipient | No relationship, competitor, anonymous, dark web | Existing relationship but missing authorization | | Intent | Deliberate exfiltration, concealment, fraud | Negligent, pressure-driven, self-serving | | Sender signals | Departure + competitor, financial distress | Departure alone, work pressure, career ambition | | Reversibility | Irreversible (data in the wild, keys compromised) | Potentially recoverable (known recipient, NDA) | HIGH vs. MEDIUM: | Factor | Tips toward HIGH | Tips toward MEDIUM | | Recipient | External (competitor, vendor, public) | Internal (wrong department, over-broad access) | | Sender signals | Departure, grievance, financial conflict | None, or mild (eagerness, inexperience, pressure) | | Governance bypass | Deliberate, aware of the requirement | Accidental or ignorance-based | | Data volume/impact | Bulk export, complete portfolios, credentials | Limited scope, single documents, operational data | | Concealment | Some evidence (off-hours, encrypted, mislabeling) | None (sender often openly discusses the sharing) | MEDIUM vs. LOW: | Factor | Tips toward MEDIUM | Tips toward LOW | | Authorization | Missing or incomplete | Fully authorized | | Content sensitivity | Contains sensitive data that should not be shared | Already public, personal, or designed for distribution| | Process compliance | Process was violated | Process was followed | | Risk factors | Mild but present | None | --- ## Amplifiers and Mitigators Amplifiers (push severity UP): - Confirmed departure to competitor - Financial distress or personal grievance - Deliberate concealment (encryption to bypass DLP, mislabeled files, off-hours timing) - Pattern of escalation (multiple incidents, increasing volume) - Undisclosed conflicts of interest - Prior disciplinary history for similar behavior Mitigators (push severity DOWN): - Recipient has existing authorized relationship (vendor with NDA, engaged law firm) - Data stayed within organizational systems (self-email, internal misdirection) - Clear accidental nature (autocomplete error, wrong channel) - Sender proactively disclosed or sought approval - Data volume is limited (single document vs. bulk export) - Content was already public or approaching public release --- Respond in this exact JSON format only, no other text: {"severity": "CRITICAL", "analysis": "Your WHO/WHAT/WHO reasoning here"} --- ### Sender Context - Name: {sender_name} - Role: {sender_role} - Department: {sender_dept} - Tenure: {sender_tenure} - Location: {sender_location} - Risk Factors: {sender_risks} ### Recipient Context - Name: {recipient_name} - Organization: {recipient_org} - Relationship: {recipient_rel} ### Email From: {email_from} To: {email_to} CC: {email_cc} (only if present) BCC: {email_bcc} (only if present) Date: {email_date} Subject: {email_subject} Attachments: {email_attachments} (only if present) {email_body}

What Changed Between Prompts

Both prompts share the same role setup, WHO/WHAT/WHO framework, JSON response format, and sender/recipient/email data injection. The only difference is the severity guidance section.
Dimension Minimal Prompt Detailed Prompt
Severity Definitions 4 one-line definitions
~40 words		 Full descriptions with core patterns, violation categories, and key signals per tier
~800 words
Violation Categories Not provided 7 categories for Critical, 7 for High, 7 for Medium, 6 for Low — each with concrete examples of what it looks like
Boundary Decisions Not provided Three decision matrices (Critical/High, High/Medium, Medium/Low) with 4-6 factors each and concrete "tips toward" guidance
Amplifiers & Mitigators Not provided 6 amplifiers and 6 mitigators that adjust severity based on contextual signals
Combinatorial Guidance Not provided "Severity emerges from the COMBINATION of these dimensions. The same data sent to different recipients can be different severities."
Total Prompt Length ~120 words (fixed) + email data ~1,800 words (fixed) + email data

Impact on Results

Model Minimal Accuracy Detailed Accuracy Improvement
Claude Sonnet 4 72.4% 87.3% +14.9%
GPT-4.1 67.3% 80.7% +13.3%
Gemini 2.5 Pro 60.7% 74.7% +14.0%
GPT-4.1 Mini 59.3% 60.0% +0.7%
Gemini 2.5 Flash 50.7% 50.0% -0.7%
Key finding: The detailed prompt's greatest impact was on the HIGH and MEDIUM tiers, where models without guidance collapse to binary thinking ("bad" = Critical, "not bad" = Low). HIGH accuracy jumped from 24.5% average to 53.5% (+29%). Efficient models (Mini, Flash) showed near-zero improvement, suggesting a capacity ceiling for processing complex classification frameworks.
